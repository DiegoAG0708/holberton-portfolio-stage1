2. Research and Brainstorming
We started by doing individual research before getting together as a group. Each of us spent time thinking about problems people deal with in everyday life — things that are annoying, inefficient, or just not solved well yet. We also used AI as a brainstorming tool to help us generate a broad list of ideas quickly. We want to be transparent about that — we did not use it to make decisions or write anything, just to get a starting list of possibilities on the table faster than we could have done alone. From there, everything was our own discussion, evaluation, and judgment.
Once we had a pool of ideas to work with, we got together and used a few techniques to make sense of them.
Mind Mapping: We sketched out a rough mind map, grouping ideas into categories like "local services," "student tools," "health," and "transportation." Each category branched into more specific directions. This helped us see which ideas had enoug  depth to actually build something around and which ones were too vague or too similar to something that already exists.
SCAMPER Framework: For the ideas that seemed most interesting we asked SCAMPER questions — could we substitute part of how something already works? Could we combine two ideas? Could we adapt something that exists in another country to our local context here in Mexico? This pushed us past the obvious first version of each idea.
"How Might We" Questions: This turned out to be the most useful technique for us. Instead of starting with a solution, we started with a problem and asked how we might solve it. For example: "How might we help someone find a trustworthy person to fix something in their house without spending hours asking around?" That question almost immediately pointed us toward FixIt, and the answer felt natural enough that we knew we were onto something worth exploring.

3. Ideas Explored
We ended up with 8 ideas total. Here is each one with an honest breakdown of what we liked about it and why we ended up moving on.

Idea 1 — FixIt: Neighborhood Services Marketplace
A platform where people post small jobs they need done and local skilled people respond with offers to help. Think repairs, cleaning, furniture assembly, painting — the kind of thing where you spend more time trying to find someone than the job actually takes.

Strengths: Solves a real and visible problem, works with the skills we have, clear way to make money through commissions, strong community impact.
Weaknesses: Requires users on both sides from the start, trust and safety need to be handled carefully.
Decision: Selected as our MVP. Full breakdown in Section 4.


Idea 2 — MediTrack: Medication Reminder App
An app to help people track their medications, set reminders, and log doctor appointments.

Strengths: Genuinely useful especially for older adults, straightforward concept, freemium model makes sense.
Weaknesses: Handling health data responsibly is complicated. There are privacy and ethical considerations that go well beyond what we are equipped to deal with at this stage. Also, apps like Medisafe already do this well and for free.
Decision: Rejected. The combination of existing competition and the responsibility that comes with health data made this the wrong choice for us right now.


Idea 3 — LocalHarvest: Farmer-to-Buyer Platform
Connect small local farmers and home gardeners directly with buyers in the community, cutting out the middleman and keeping money local.

Strengths: Real social and economic impact, especially in Mexico where small farmers often struggle to reach buyers. Good monetization through transaction fees.
Weaknesses: You need farmers and buyers using the platform at the same time for it to work. Getting both sides onboard at launch without a marketing budget is really difficult. Digital literacy among small farmers is also a genuine barrier.
Decision: Rejected. The two-sided launch problem felt too risky for a first project. We liked the idea but felt it needed more resources than we have.


Idea 4 — PeerCode: Student Coding Help Platform
A place where students post coding questions and other students answer them, with a reputation and points system to reward good answers.

Strengths: We understand the problem firsthand. Directly useful to people like us. Could build community among students.
Weaknesses: Stack Overflow exists and is free and massive. We spent a lot of time trying to figure out what we would do differently and could not come up with a convincing answer.
Decision: Rejected. Without a clear differentiator from what already exists, there is no realistic reason for someone to use ours instead.


Idea 5 — BusAlert: Crowdsourced Bus Tracker
Passengers report bus arrivals and delays in real time so other people waiting at stops know what to expect.

Strengths: Anyone who takes public transit in a city with unreliable schedules would use this. Wide audience, clear daily use case.
Weaknesses: The app is useless until a lot of people are actively contributing data at the same time. Getting that critical mass with no budget and no existing user base felt like a wall we could not get past early on.
Decision: Rejected. The cold start problem was too serious. The product has no value until it already has users, which makes launching nearly impossible from scratch.


Idea 6 — SplitEasy: Group Expense Splitter
Track shared expenses among roommates or travel groups, log who paid what, and send reminders to settle up.

Strengths: Everyone has dealt with this problem. Easy to understand, easy to explain to users.
Weaknesses: Splitwise already does this and is free and well-designed. We genuinely could not identify what we would offer that Splitwise does not.
Decision: Rejected. Competing with a free, established, and well-loved product without a strong differentiator does not make sense.


Idea 7 — StudySync: Collaborative Study Tool
Study groups with shared notes, scheduling tools, and quizzes built in.

Strengths: Relevant to students, promotes collaboration, could integrate with school calendars.
Weaknesses: People already use WhatsApp, Notion, Google Docs, and Discord for this. The idea felt like a solution to a problem that people have already solved well enough with free tools they already use.
Decision: Rejected. No compelling reason for students to switch to a new platform when they are already comfortable with what they have.


Idea 8 — LangBuddy: Language Exchange App
Pairs people who want to learn each other's languages and connects them for regular conversation practice.

Strengths: Promotes cultural exchange, genuine demand proven by Tandem and HelloTalk.
Weaknesses: Those existing apps have large active user bases we cannot compete with. Safety is also a real concern — connecting strangers for video conversations requires solid moderation tools we are not ready to build.
Decision: Rejected. Between the competition and the safety requirements, this was not the right fit for our team at this stage.

4. Selected MVP — FixIt
What it is
FixIt is a web platform where people can post small jobs they need done around the house or office, and local people with the relevant skills can browse those posts and offer to help. Once both sides agree on the job and the price, the work gets done and both users leave a review. Simple as that.
The kinds of jobs we are thinking about: fixing a leaking pipe, assembling flat-pack furniture, painting a room, moving boxes, basic electrical work, cleaning. Things that people need done regularly but have no reliable way to find help for.
The problem it solves
Finding a trustworthy handyman is genuinely painful. You ask a friend, they give you a number, the person does not show up or charges double what they said. On the other side, there are people in every neighborhood who are good at exactly these things and want the work, but their only marketing is word of mouth. FixIt creates a space where both sides can find each other reliably, with reviews and accountability built in.
Who it is for
On one side: renters, homeowners, and small business owners who need things fixed or done and want to find someone reliable without spending hours searching.
On the other side: skilled individuals — plumbers, carpenters, electricians, painters, cleaners — who want a steady way to find work and build a reputation beyond their immediate social circle.
What the MVP will actually include
We kept the scope realistic for where we are in the course:

User registration and login for both job posters and service providers
A form to post a job with category, description, location, and budget
A browsable feed of open jobs for service providers
A way for providers to send an offer or express interest in a job
Basic in-app messaging between the two users once there is a match
A rating and review system after a job is completed
A clean, simple interface built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript
A Python backend to handle users, jobs, and data

We are not including a payment system in the MVP. Users will arrange payment between themselves. This keeps the scope manageable and avoids the legal and technical complexity of handling real transactions at this stage.
Why we chose this
Honestly the simplest reason is that it felt real to all of us. We have all had the experience of needing something fixed and not knowing who to call, or knowing someone who does this kind of work but struggles to find clients. It is not a problem we read about; it is one we have seen.
Beyond that, it fits what we can actually build right now. The core features map directly to what we are learning in the course; forms, user accounts, a database, a backend, a frontend. We are not trying to use machine learning or integrate a dozen APIs. The challenge is in building something clean and functional, which is exactly the kind of challenge this stage of the course is designed for.
And there is a real business model here. Taking a small commission on completed jobs is proven, simple, and does not require users to pay anything upfront. The platform only makes money when it actually creates value.
Potential challenges
Trust: People are letting someone into their home. The review system needs to work well from the beginning. We will also think about simple safety features like verified profiles.
Getting both sides at once: A marketplace needs supply and demand to be useful. Our plan is to start in one specific area rather than trying to launch everywhere at once. Small and focused is better than broad and empty.
No payment system yet: Asking users to handle payment themselves is a limitation. It works for an MVP but will need to be addressed before any real launch.
Opportunities
There is no dominant platform doing this at a local level in most Latin American cities. The informal economy for these kinds of services is huge; the demand is already there, it just has no good home online yet. If FixIt works in one city, expanding is a natural next step. Partnerships with local hardware stores or vocational training centers could also add real value to service providers using the platform.

5. Process Summary
We started individually, came together, argued a bit about a few ideas, scored everything honestly, and landed on FixIt unanimously. The process took a couple of sessions (two sessions). Nobody felt pushed into the decision, meaning it was the idea that kept making the most sense every time we came back to it.
We think there is a real version of this that works for our community, and building it well is a challenge worth taking on.
